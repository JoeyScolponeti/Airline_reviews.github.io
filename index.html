Overall Introduction

Many consumers have had poor experiences with commercial airlines, especially after the COVID-19 pandemic disrupted travel possibilities for consumers around the world. There are a number of factors that contribute to negative travel experiences, including poor service, delayed flights, uncomfortable in-flight seating, lost luggage, and long wait times. With so many airline options available to the public, it is important for consumers to understand their options, and which airlines will (and won’t) satisfy their travel needs. By conducting a thorough analysis of our dataset of airline reviews, we can gain valuable insights into consumer airline experiences that have the potential to disrupt the entire global airline industry worth $726.8 billion. Not only does this data exploration have significant financial implications, but it can also serve as a starting point for addressing core issues within the travel industry. It can improve airline services, streamline business operations, and perhaps most importantly, boost customer satisfaction in years to come. After cleaning the dataset, performing detailed sentiment analysis, and creating interactive visualizations, this analysis aims to identify why airline reviews receive the rating that they get. This helps the industry through pinpointing areas of improvement, as well as assisting the consumer by breaking down the context of reviews.

More information about the driving force behind negative airline reviews, can be found at: “Here’s How Much United Airlines Stock Tanked This Week.”

Or, explore the article about passenger complaints about airline travel in 2023: CBS News.

The Dataset

The dataset we used for this project is named “SkyRatings: Unleashing 23K+ Airline Reviews!”, which can be found on Kaggle. The reviews were scraped from www.airlinequality.com, which is an open forum where passengers can share their flight experiences to help other passengers make informed booking decisions. The original data contained 23,172 reviews. Depending on the visualization, the dataset was cleaned and filtered to best fit the attributes being analyzed. The attributes are as follows:

  • Airline Name
  • Overall Rating
    • The rating of the reviewer's experience with the airline between 1 and 9
  • Review Title
  • Review Date
  • Verified
    • Boolean value indicating in the reviewer is a verified user
  • Review
    • Full string of the written review for the airline
  • Aircraft
  • Type Of Traveller
    • One of four types of travellers:
      • Business
      • Couple Leisure
      • Family Leisure
      • Solo Leisure
  • Seat Type
    • Economy, Business, First Class, etc.
  • Route
  • Date Flown
    • Month and year that trip was taken
  • Recommended
    • Boolean value representing if the reviewer would recommend this airline to others

The remaining attributes are "subscores" of the overall rating, meaning that the reviewer also rated the quality of more specific parts of their flight. Unlike with Overall Rating, all of these ratings are between 1 and 5. The categories are:

  • Seat Comfort
  • Cabin Staff Service
  • Food & Beverages
  • Ground Service
  • Inflight Entertainment
  • Wifi & Connectivity
  • Value For Money

Subscore Visualizations

Composition of Overall Ratings

The area plot displayed above showcases the distribution of Overall Rating values. Additionally, the data is color-coded by rating sentiment, which can be "Positive" (ratings of 7 to 9), "Neutral" (ratings of 4 to 6), or "Negative" (ratings of 1 to 3). The plot's tooltip gives more specific information on each individual ratings frequency. One evident fact that it helps to highlight is that "1" ratings are the most common by far. There are ~11,000 of them, while almost every other bucket has less than 2,000. In other words, a majority of the reviews are extremely harsh, which makes sense since customers are more likely to leave a review if they were wronged by the airline, and want to warn others before booking. "Neutral" reviews have the fewest ratings, which is logical because if customers don't care as much about an airline, they’re less inclined to provide their opinion on it. "Positive" reviews are slightly more frequent than "Neutral" ones, which is intriguing because you would believe that if people were passionate about a trip, you would see similar frequencies to the negative reviews. This is likely because people only really care about the quality of their flight when things go horribly wrong. Therefore, from the airline's perspective, it's critical that issues with the services are addressed first before looking to improve aspects that are already good. Also, because of this skew, potential traveller's should view these ratings as evaluation of how little issues they had with their trip.

Importance of Subscores based on Traveller Classification

This bar plot compares each kind of traveller's ratings towards each different subscore. If airlines were looking to appeal to a given market segment, this visualization would help to identify where each traveller type's values lie. By layering the types of travellers side-by-side in this fashion, two distinct groupings can be seen. "Business" and "Solo Leisure" share very similar ratings, as well as "Couple Leisure" and "Family Leisure". Coincidentally, both of these groups can be boiled down to solo travellers and group travellers. With this correlation in mind, it shows that solo travellers have higher ratings across the board compared to than group travellers, implying that they are less critical towards their flight experience. This is supported by the point identified in the previous graph that overall ratings are mostly influenced by those hard-hitting negative reviews. Even with these individual differences, it's clear that all of these groups are following a similar trend to which aspects of the flight they care about the most. This graph points out that passengers have the worst experiences with airline wi-fi relative to everything else. Most people turn to their phones and computers when they are on a plane, so if they are having connectivity issues the entire time, they are likely going to be very annoyed and leave bad reviews.

Sentiment Analysis

Sentiment analysis was conducted on airline reviews to better understand customer satisfaction. The analysis utilized sentiment scores, including positive, negative, and composite compound scores to conduct a comprehensive assessment of each review. The data revealed significant variations in sentiment across airlines, with some showing consistently positive feedback, while others exhibiting more polarized responses. Airlines with high sentiment volatility often can possibly be correlated with specific events or operational inconsistencies. Below are two interactive visualization of the data collected from this analysis:

Sentiment Trends over Time

This plot displayed the compound sentiment scores for various airlines over time. The filter displays one airline at a time, and any airline in the dataset can be selected to display their trend. By highlighting changes in sentiment, the line chart can enable stakeholders to identify specific periods of heightened customer dissatisfaction or approval. For instance, a sudden drop in sentiment could signal operational issues, such as flight delays or cancellations, while a peak might indicate successful promotional campaigns or exceptional service experiences. It was expected that the trends shown by these views would be linear and flat, however, many of the major airlines have trends that shoot erratically up and down on sentiment. There are a couple of likely reasons for this. First off, given the size of the dataset (~23,000 reviews) compared to the number of airlines present (~500), if the reviews were hypothetically spread evenly, there would only be about 46 reviews per airline. That number of datapoints is not sufficient to show a smooth curve across timelines that sometimes span over a decade. However, since these graph are showcasing individual reviews, it goes to show that flight experience overall is very volatile in general. Some people have a normal flight with no issues, while other people can fly the same airline and have terrible things happen to them. Therefore, it's important to recognize the pitfalls in generalizing the quality of an airline.

Word Cloud

The Word Cloud Visualization highlights the most frequently used words in the airline reviews for both positive and negative reviews. Either category can be selected with the filter at the bottom, and hovering over each word gives their frequency within the entire dataset. Larger words represent terms that appear more often, offering insights into common themes, concerns, or praises. On the positive side, some of the larger words identify things that went will in these reviews, such as good service, comfortable seating, etc. Negative reviews mention delays, cancelled flights, and extra charges. These give an idea for the reasoning that each kind of review are coming in, and given the conclusions derived earlier, focusing in on those negative words can help airlines improve their overall quality.

Summary and Takeaways

This analysis shed some interesting light on how to go about booking trips. While it's tempting to look at one big "overall" review and pick the highest number, there is a lot more nuance to the decision. Different airlines excel in different areas, and it's important to select one that aligns with your values the best. The further down you dig into the details of each of these reviews, the more context you will find for why airlines are rated the way they are. The most important information will always lie within the individual reviews, since the progression of breaking down these reviews bit by bit put into frame just how much detail you lose when rounding up to one big score. When looking at this analysis on the business perspective, the takeaway is clear: reduce the pain on customers before improving fields that are already sufficient. The point of the transportation industry is to get people from point A to B with as little headache as possible, and that fact is supported by what was shown here. The fewer obstacles placed in front of the consumer, the better experience they'll have, and with that comes higher ratings which correlate with more bookings.

Through the development of this project, some improvements are clear. As hinted at earlier, a larger dataset is vital in putting more weight behind the messages of these visualizations. Not only would more records smoothen out the curves, but more features in the dataset could help us come to even more useful conclusions that can help both the industry and the consumer. For example, rolling in ticket price can greatly impact how airline recommendations are made. The experience could be very good, but it might not warrant an abhorrent ticket price. Especially when ticket prices are easily the most considered value when it comes to booking flights, performing this study without that information proved difficult. However, it's very likely that that data is difficult to come across.
